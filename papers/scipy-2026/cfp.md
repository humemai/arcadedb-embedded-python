# SciPy 2026 Call for Proposals

**Submission deadline:** February 25, 2026 (AOE)

The 25th annual SciPy conference will be held at the University of Minnesota, **July 13–19, 2026**. The conference brings together participants from industry, academia, and government to showcase projects, learn from skilled users and developers, and collaborate on code development.

## Conference Schedule

- **Tutorials:** July 13–14 (2 days)
- **General Conference:** July 15–17 (3 days)
- **Developer Sprints:** July 18–19 (2 days)

---

## Talk & Poster Presentations

### Highlighted Tracks

#### Spirit of SciPy

Twenty-five years ago, the first SciPy conference convened scientists eager to solve open problems with Python. As the community and conference have grown, that spirit remains central. This track celebrates open-source software and collaborative learning, sharing, and discovery.

**We invite submissions that showcase:**

- Ground-up collaborations
- Interdisciplinary uses or unconventional repurposing of domain-specific tools
- Projects and organizations that trace their roots to SciPy

#### Data-Driven Discovery, Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence

This track brings together advances in AI/ML and data-driven insights that enable novel discovery across science and industry. It includes development and application of open-source tools and techniques that advance scientific progress.

**Submissions are especially encouraged for:**

- Applications of AI/ML in scientific workflows
- Improvements to simulation and simulation-based inference
- New or existing open-source methods that enable discovery

### Additional Conference Tracks

#### General

This track showcases tools and projects highlighting broad applicability of Python in science, including open-source development in the SciPy ecosystem. It is also the right home for interdisciplinary work and proposals that do not fit domain-specific tracks.

#### Physics and Astronomy

Topics include (but are not limited to):

- Data analysis
- Simulation
- Visualization in experimental and theoretical physics and astronomy

This year, submissions related to quantum information science are especially encouraged, including quantum computing, simulation, and control.

#### Environmental, Earth, and Climate Sciences

This track welcomes Python applications that help understand, model, and solve problems in earth, ocean, geo, ecological, environmental, climate, and atmospheric science and engineering.

#### Maintainers and Community

This track covers work and challenges in the maintainer community, including:

- Building organizations
- Launching major technical changes
- Maintaining open-source projects
- Growing contributor and user communities

Speakers are encouraged to go beyond project updates and share transferable lessons for others building open-source projects and communities.

#### Biological and Medical Sciences

Topics include (but are not limited to):

- Computational biology
- Medical imaging
- Health data analysis
- Bioinformatics
- Medical instruments and laboratory automation

We welcome submissions showing how Python advances research, discovery, and innovation in living systems and human health.

#### Scientific Computing in Education

This track highlights scientific computing in academia, in both teaching and research.

**Key topics include:**

- Scientific computing in the classroom
- Tools for teaching and learning
- Academic research using the Python ecosystem

Student speaker submissions are particularly encouraged.

### Not sure where your talk fits?

If your tool applies to a broad audience, spans many domains, or does not clearly fit another track, submit to the **General** track.

## What You Need to Submit (Talk/Poster)

### Submission deadline

Proposals must be submitted by **February 25, 2026 (AOE)**.

### Abstract

Your abstract appears in the online schedule and should be around **100 words or less**.

### Description

Your description is used for program placement and should be roughly **500 words**. It should concisely describe:

- Software of interest to the SciPy community
- Tools or techniques for more effective computing
- How scientific Python was applied to solve a research problem

A traditional background/motivation, methods, results, and conclusion structure is encouraged (but not required).

**Helpful additions:**

- Links to project websites and source repositories
- Figures and full papers
- Evidence of public speaking ability

### Format selection

You must choose whether to submit as a **talk**, **tutorial**, or **poster**.

- Talks not selected are automatically considered for a poster slot.
- A virtual-only poster is possible.

### Optional paper upload

The form allows you to upload a paper, but this is optional and **not required** to submit a talk or poster.

## Tips for Submitting a Proposal

SciPy receives many excellent proposals and has limited speaking slots. To improve your chances:

1. Submit early (you can keep editing up to the deadline).
2. Clearly identify your intended audience and concrete learning outcomes.
3. Ensure relevance to a broad SciPy audience.
4. Include links to source code, articles, blog posts, or related writing.
5. If you have presented before, include links to slides/videos.
6. Remember timing: SciPy talks are **25 minutes** plus **2–3 minutes** for questions.
7. Avoid product-commercial framing; focus on problem-solving and open-source value.

Many of these tips are adapted from PyCon Proposal Resources (thanks PSF).

If your proposal is not accepted, you may still give a lightning talk, host a Birds of a Feather session, or discuss poster options with the Program Committee.

## How Proposals Are Reviewed and Selected

Talks, posters, and tutorials follow a similar process with **open reviews** (submitter and reviewer identities are open).

### Review assignment and scoring

Submissions are assigned to reviewers with domain expertise. Each submission is reviewed by at least **3 reviewers** and scored on:

- **Overall evaluation:** numerical score -2 to 2 + text evaluation
- **Broad community interest:** numerical score -2 to 2
- **Clarity:** numerical score -2 to 2
- **Completeness:** numerical score -2 to 2
- **Relevance and immediate usefulness:** numerical score -2 to 2

### Scheduling workflow

- Track Chairs review abstracts, scores, and comments and provide recommendations.
- Program Committee Co-Chairs build the initial conference schedule.
- Accepted talk/poster submitters are contacted to confirm attendance.
- A second tier of talks is identified in case initial selections cannot attend.
- Tutorial Co-Chairs build the tutorial schedule, balancing level (beginner/intermediate/advanced) and topic diversity.

Questions about process: **community@scipy.org**

## Proceedings

### Proceedings submission

Once your talk is accepted, presenters may submit a paper of up to **8 pages** by **June 6** for the SciPy 2026 Proceedings.

- Papers should follow the abstract/description spirit and provide deeper detail.
- Each paper is reviewed by **2 reviewers** assigned by the Proceedings Committee.
- Authors are expected to incorporate reviewer feedback.
- Publication occurs after reviewer and editor approval.

Submitting a proceedings paper implies consent to publication and DOI assignment. Even if you do not plan to submit a paper, consider volunteering as a reviewer.

## Tutorials

### Topics and format

Tutorials should focus on a well-defined topic in a **hands-on** manner. At least **50%** of time should be hands-on exercises where possible. Tutorials are **4 hours** long.

In your tutorial application, specify:

- Prerequisite skills and knowledge
- Expected learner level (beginner, intermediate, advanced)

SciPy is looking for engaging techniques and packages that help Python users build better or faster scientific applications.

### Information for tutorial presenters

#### Selection

- Accepted tutorials announced: **late March**
- Draft instructions and materials due: **June 12**
- Final tutorial materials due: **June 28**

Draft/final materials should include tested setup instructions, including final software version requirements. If no installation is required due to a provided runtime environment, state that explicitly. A pre-tutorial Slack channel will be available, and presenters are expected to support setup questions.

#### Materials

All tutorial materials (notebooks, slides, etc.) should be available to attendees before the tutorial.

#### Stipend

SciPy pays a **$1,000 stipend** to each instructor (or instructor team) for each half-day session led.

### Tutorial submission checklist

Provide:

- A short bio for presenter(s), including training/teaching/speaking experience and links to videos when available
- Prerequisite skill list for attendees
- A public-facing tutorial description (target audience, expected background, and goals)
- A detailed tutorial outline with timing and exercise sessions
- Detailed installation instructions for common Python environments
- Tutorial notes/slides/exercise files/IPython notebooks, if available (preliminary material is acceptable)
